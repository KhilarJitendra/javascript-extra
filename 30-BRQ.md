## 1. Challenging Task

**1. Tell me about a challenging technical problem you faced**

One of the biggest challenges I handled was improving performance in a large luxury e-commerce checkout flow. The page had too many things loading at once, so the experience felt slow. I worked on code splitting, lazy loading, and reducing unnecessary API calls. After that, the site became much faster and more stable for users. 

**2. Tell me about the most complex feature you worked on**

The most complex feature I worked on was the multi-step checkout flow for an international e-commerce platform. It had address handling for 15+ countries, 20+ shipping methods, and multiple payment options like Adyen, PayPal, Apple Pay, Google Pay, WeChat Pay, and Alipay. I worked closely with product, backend, and design teams to make sure the flow was smooth and localized. 

**3. Tell me about learning something quickly**

I had to quickly get deep into Next.js-related implementation details while building scalable frontend features. I studied the documentation, tried small experiments, and then applied it in the project step by step. That helped me deliver the feature confidently without slowing down the team. 

**4. Tell me about a difficult bug**

In a complex checkout flow, I faced an issue where the UI behavior was not matching the expected state after API updates. I traced the flow carefully, checked the data coming from the backend, and fixed the frontend state handling. After that, the issue was stable, and I also added checks so it would not come back again. 

**5. Tell me about unclear requirements**

Sometimes the product requirement was not fully clear at the start, especially in UI-heavy features. In that case, I discussed the details with the product and design teams, asked the right questions, and aligned on the expected flow before coding. That saved time and reduced rework later. 

**6. Tell me about designing from scratch**

I have designed reusable component systems using a component-driven approach. At AKQA, I helped build and maintain a large monorepo with 150+ reusable React components and 70+ custom hooks. That made the codebase more scalable, consistent, and easier for the team to reuse. 

## 2. Conflict

**7. Tell me about a disagreement with your manager**

I once had a discussion where delivery was being pushed very fast, but I felt performance should not be ignored. I shared the impact clearly and backed it with practical results from the project. In the end, we aligned on delivering fast without compromising user experience. 

**8. Tell me about a conflict with a team member**

Sometimes backend changes affected the frontend flow without enough advance notice. In such cases, I spoke directly with the team member, explained the impact on the user flow, and aligned on the API contract. That helped both sides move faster with fewer surprises. 

**9. Tell me about a code review disagreement**

During code review, there were cases where I and the reviewer had different views on the best approach. I listened to the concerns, compared the trade-offs, and then chose the option that was better for maintainability and the product. If needed, I used a hybrid solution that balanced both sides. 

**10. Tell me about convincing someone technically**

I had to convince the team to use performance improvements like lazy loading and code splitting in a large e-commerce app. I explained the real user impact and showed how it could improve the experience. Since the argument was based on performance and user value, the team agreed. 

**11. Tell me about handling a difficult stakeholder**

When timelines were tight, I broke the work into smaller phases and focused first on the most important part. I kept communication clear and realistic, so the stakeholder knew what would be delivered first and what would come later. That helped manage expectations well. 

**12. Tell me about a frontend and backend disagreement**

A common challenge was making sure frontend and backend stayed aligned on data structure and integration behavior. I handled this by discussing the API contract early and keeping communication open during development. That reduced integration issues and made the final flow more stable. 

## 3. Time Management

**13. Tell me about working with a tight deadline**

When the timeline was short, I focused on the most important user journey first and reused existing components wherever possible. I also avoided unnecessary work and kept the implementation simple. That helped me deliver on time without losing quality. 

**14. Tell me about handling multiple tasks at once**

I usually start by prioritizing tasks based on business impact and urgency. Then I split the work into smaller pieces and track progress carefully. This helps me stay organized even when several tasks are running together. 

**15. Tell me about prioritization**

If a production issue comes in, I treat it as the top priority because it affects users directly. I pause less urgent work, fix the issue first, and then return to the planned tasks. This is the right balance between speed and responsibility. 

**16. Tell me about working under pressure**

I have worked on large, high-scale e-commerce features where timelines were strict and quality still mattered. In such cases, I stay calm, communicate early, and work closely with the team to remove blockers. That helps me deliver under pressure without rushing blindly. 

**17. Tell me about delivering quickly**

Reusable components helped me deliver features faster because I did not need to build everything from scratch. Since I worked on a monorepo with many shared components, I could move quickly while still keeping consistency. That saved a lot of development time. 

**18. Tell me about a production issue**

When a production issue happens, I first focus on restoring stability quickly. I investigate the root cause, fix or roll back if needed, and then verify the solution carefully before closing it. After that, I add preventive steps so the same issue does not repeat. 

## 4. Ownership

**19. Tell me about ownership beyond your role**

I often took ownership of frontend work end-to-end, not just the UI part. That included architecture, component structure, performance, testing, and coordination with other teams. I like to make sure the feature is complete and usable, not only coded. 

**20. Tell me about mentoring**

I have helped junior developers by reviewing their code and guiding them on structure, consistency, and best practices. I try to explain not just what to change, but why it matters. That helps them learn faster and work more independently. 

**21. Tell me about improving a process**

I helped improve code quality by introducing standards like ESLint, Prettier, and Husky. This made the codebase more consistent and reduced small mistakes before they reached production. It also made team collaboration easier. 

**22. Tell me about a performance improvement**

Performance improvement has been a big part of my work. At AKQA, I used code splitting, lazy loading, PWA techniques, service workers, and caching strategies to achieve 90+ Lighthouse scores. This made the experience much smoother for users. 

**23. Tell me about helping another team**

I have worked with designers, backend engineers, and product stakeholders many times to solve delivery issues. When another team needed frontend input, I joined early, explained the impact clearly, and helped align the solution. That made cross-team work smoother. 

**24. Tell me about leading a feature**

I have led frontend work for critical user flows, especially in the checkout journey. I usually take responsibility for the structure, implementation, quality, and delivery of the feature. I stay involved until the feature is stable and ready for users. 

## 5. Failure

**25. Tell me about a time you missed a deadline**

If a delay happens because of an external dependency, I inform stakeholders early instead of waiting until the end. I then adjust the plan, continue with what I can control, and make sure the final delivery is still strong. Clear communication is very important in such cases. 

**26. Tell me about a wrong estimation**

Sometimes a feature looks simpler at first, but later you realize it has more edge cases or integration work. In that situation, I update the estimate quickly and communicate the reason honestly. That helps everyone plan better. 

**27. Tell me about a timeline change**

When new requirements come in during development, I reassess the work and re-plan the tasks. I focus on the most important part first and keep the team informed about what will change. That keeps the project realistic. 

**28. Tell me about being blocked by a dependency**

When I am blocked by backend or design dependency, I continue with mock data, reusable components, or other tasks I can complete in parallel. This keeps progress moving instead of waiting idle. It also reduces delivery risk. 

**29. Tell me about a release delay**

If a bug is found before release, I prefer to delay slightly rather than ship a bad experience. I would rather fix the issue properly and protect the user experience. In frontend work, quality matters a lot. 

**30. Tell me about a mistake and what you learned**

One thing I learned over time is that even good code should be checked for performance and quality before release. In large frontend apps, small inefficiencies can affect the user experience. Now I always think about testing, performance, and maintainability before I finish a feature.
